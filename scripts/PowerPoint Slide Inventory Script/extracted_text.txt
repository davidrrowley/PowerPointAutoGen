Section 3 – Scenario 1 – Customer Platform
Discovery and Shaping Approach
How you would scope and shape the next 18-24 months of the Customer Platform roadmap
How discovery would inform business case development and investment decisions
How you ensure traceability from user needs to architectural and commercial decisions
IBM would begin the Customer Platform programme with a structured discovery phase designed to establish a clear roadmap for the next 18 to 24 months while ensuring that investment decisions are grounded in validated user need, technical evidence and delivery realism. Discovery would be delivered through a blended IBM and Stable team, ensuring close engagement with NRW stakeholders in Wales and grounding the roadmap in NRW’s operational context from the outset. The phase is treated not only as a shaping exercise, but also as a controlled mobilisation step that establishes delivery readiness, governance and ways of working early.
The discovery phase is deliberately structured around three parallel activities.  First, user and stakeholder discovery, working with NRW teams, operational users and external stakeholders to understand how services are currently accessed, where friction exists and which user journeys create the greatest operational or customer pain. User research, service mapping, analytics and direct observation are used to identify priority opportunities for improving customer interactions across the platform.
Second, technical and architectural assessment spanning the ecosystem components such as Dynamics 365, Power Platform, SharePoint and Azure services, alongside existing CMS, data and integration capabilities. The aim is to establish a clear baseline architecture, identify technical constraints and determine where modernisation, integration uplift or platform consolidation would provide the greatest benefit.
Third, commercial and delivery assessment establishes programme phases, delivery models, sequencing options and key investment assumptions, while exposing cost drivers and delivery dependencies early. This enables NRW to take staged, evidence-based investment decisions rather than committing upfront to a fully specified programme.
Discovery outputs would typically include:
A prioritised 18-24 month service roadmap aligned to NRW’s desired outcomes (single customer portal, consolidated customer account, omni-channel engagement, AI-enabled interactions, and the Product Delivery CoE etc.)
An architectural direction aligned with the Microsoft ecosystem.
A platform capability model describing required services and integrations.
A sequenced delivery plan supporting business case development
A delivery mobilisation view covering governance, team shape and knowledge transfer approach
Traceability from user needs through to architectural and investment decisions would be maintained through structured artefacts such as user journeys, service blueprints, capability maps, architecture decision records and outcome-based prioritisation frameworks, ensuring that prioritisation and funding decisions remain directly anchored to validated user and organisational needs.
The discovery phase would therefore provide Natural Resources Wales with a clear, evidence-based foundation for investment decisions and future platform evolution.
Foundational Architectural Direction
High-level view of how the Customer Platform should evolve within the Microsoft ecosystem (Dynamics 365, Power Platform, Azure, SharePoint)
How CMS, CRM and integration components should be treated as foundational building blocks
Key architectural principles you would apply (not detailed designs)
The Customer Platform should be evolved as a cohesive, product-aligned platform built on NRW’s existing Microsoft estate, rather than as a set of loosely connected solutions. The current baseline is already Azure-centric, with established use of Dynamics 365, Power Platform and Microsoft 365. The architectural task is therefore to organise these capabilities into a layered, Microsoft-aligned ecosystem, with clear separation of responsibility and controlled integration between components.
At a high level, four logical layers should structure the platform.
Customer Engagement Layer - provides the primary interface for citizens, businesses and partners. This includes portals, web services and content platforms. Power Pages should support transactional journeys, while CMS capabilities and SharePoint should manage structured content, publishing and document-centric interactions. This layer should remain decoupled from business logic, consuming services through APIs.
Customer Relationship and Service Management Layer is centred on Dynamics 365. This acts as the system of record for customer identity, interaction history and service lifecycle, supporting case management and operational workflows. It establishes a single, authoritative customer domain and should not be bypassed by channel-specific implementations.
Integration and Platform Services Layer forms the control plane of the architecture. Delivered through Azure integration services, it exposes APIs, manages service orchestration and supports event-driven communication. It enables interoperability across CRM, CMS, SharePoint, legacy systems and external services without creating point-to-point dependencies.
Data and Insights Layer underpins reporting, analytics and data sharing. Built on Azure data services, it should provide governed access to customer and operational data, with capabilities such as lineage, cataloguing and access control to ensure data remains trusted and reusable.
Within this model Dynamics 365 owns the customer domain, CMS and SharePoint own content, navigation and document management. The integration layer governs connectivity and decoupling. This separation reflects NRW’s mixed estate and enables incremental modernisation, reducing coupling between channels and back-end systems while allowing controlled evolution of data and service boundaries.  The architectural principles applied should reinforce this structure;
A cloud-first, SaaS-preferred approach should remain the default, maximising use of managed Microsoft services and limiting bespoke infrastructure. Modularity and loose coupling should guide design, ensuring clear separation between experience, business logic and integration concerns. API-first and event-driven patterns should be used consistently to support scalability and avoid point-to-point integration.
Data should be treated as a governed asset, with clear ownership, lineage and interoperability aligned to the emerging data platform. Security and privacy should be embedded from the outset, using identity-led design and zero trust principles. Observability, resilience and operability should be built in, ensuring services are monitorable and reliable in live operation. A reuse-first approach should be applied, favouring native platform capabilities and configured services ahead of custom development.
Finally, the platform should be managed as a product, with clear ownership, defined backlogs and continuous improvement. The objective is not to deliver a fixed end state, but to establish a modular, API-driven architecture that supports incremental delivery, integrates across a complex estate and provides a controlled pathway for future change.
Data Integration and Platform Thinking
Your approach to addressing fragmented customer data over time
How discovery would validate assumptions around data quality and integration complexity
Key risks and sequencing issues
Customer data fragmentation within NRW should be understood in three related forms: inconsistent customer records across systems, disconnected data flows between platforms, and fragmented user journeys where services do not present a coherent, real-time view to users. Addressing this requires both technical integration and clear control of customer identity and data ownership.
IBM’s approach (What Is Data Integration? | IBM) focuses on establishing a governed data integration framework that resolves fragmentation progressively, rather than through a high-risk, single-stage consolidation.
The first step during discovery is to map customer data across CRM platforms, operational systems, document repositories, GIS platforms and legacy applications. This identifies duplication, inconsistencies, ownership gaps and integration constraints. Particular attention is given to how customer identity is represented, including variations in identifiers and matching logic, as this is typically the root cause of fragmentation across both data and user experience.
From this baseline, a shared customer identifier model would be introduced. In practice, Dynamics 365 would act as the authoritative customer domain, assigning a unique, persistent identifier to each customer. Integration services would map existing system identifiers to this canonical ID using matching rules based on attributes such as name, address or organisation details. Where confidence thresholds are not met, records are flagged for resolution rather than merged automatically.
This establishes a logical master customer record, allowing NRW to resolve identity consistently across channels without requiring immediate physical consolidation of all data. A user interacting through the website, contacting support or progressing a case would be recognised as the same customer, enabling continuity of service and reducing fragmented journeys.
In the early stages, existing systems remain operational while integration services provide a unified interface to customer data. API-based integration and event-driven messaging ensure that updates are propagated using the shared identifier, allowing channels, CRM and operational systems to access consistent, near real-time information. This directly addresses issues such as limited integration with live data sources and inconsistent service views.
Over time, data consolidation can be introduced in a controlled manner. This includes master data management patterns, deduplication processes, survivorship rules and data quality controls, progressively improving the reliability of the customer record while reducing duplication across systems.
A trusted data platform should be established to curate customer and operational datasets for analytics and cross-service insight. In practice, this would be delivered using Azure-native services, including Microsoft Fabric, to create governed, reusable data products derived from operational systems. These datasets maintain lineage, ownership and access controls, ensuring that data remains trusted while supporting reporting and advanced analytics. This complements, rather than replaces, operational data stores such as Dataverse and line-of-business systems.
Discovery activities would validate key assumptions before wider implementation proceeds. These include data quality and completeness, integration complexity and dependency mapping, regulatory constraints and governance requirements, and the feasibility of identity matching at scale. It would also assess organisational readiness to adopt consistent data ownership and stewardship practices.
Sequencing is critical to managing risk. Identity resolution and integration capabilities should be established early, enabling consistent data exchange and improved user journeys without requiring immediate system replacement. More complex data consolidation and platform changes can then be introduced incrementally.
Key risks include poor data quality affecting matching accuracy, hidden dependencies within legacy systems, integration performance and security constraints, and inconsistent ownership of customer data across domains. There is also a dependency on organisational adoption of new governance practices and ways of working.
By combining identity-led integration, API-driven connectivity and strong governance, the Customer Platform can transition from fragmented data and disjointed journeys towards a consistent, organisation-wide customer view that supports service delivery, real-time interaction and future innovation.

Security, Testing and Service Sustainability
How security, privacy and compliance considerations would inform early discovery and design decisions
Proportionate testing and assurance principles
Approach to designing for live service, operability and continuous improvement
Knowledge transfer and capability uplift within NRW
Security, privacy and compliance inform design decisions from the outset rather than acting as downstream assurance gates. During discovery, structured threat modelling, data-flow mapping and compliance analysis establish NRW’s risk profile, regulatory obligations and the sensitivity of environmental and citizen data. These findings directly shape architectural patterns, identity models and integration controls. A security-by-design approach aligned to Zero Trust principles is applied across the platform, including strong identity verification, role-based access control, encryption in transit and at rest, and policy-driven configuration. This ensures alignment with UK GDPR, Cyber Essentials Plus and HMG Minimum Cyber Security Standards, while also addressing personnel assurance requirements such as BS7858 vetting. Privacy is embedded through data protection impact assessments, minimisation and retention strategies, and auditable least-privilege access. Formal compliance is supported through production of a Statement of Assurance, with traceable evidence derived from design and delivery activities.
Testing and assurance are proportionate to risk and data classification. High-risk components, particularly those handling personal or externally exposed data, undergo enhanced assurance including penetration testing, dependency scanning and code analysis. Lower-risk components follow a streamlined approach while still benefiting from automated functional, security and performance testing within CI/CD pipelines. Assurance activities are explicitly mapped to identified risks and control requirements, ensuring that outputs support both operational confidence and formal compliance obligations, including audit and assurance reporting.
Live-service operability is treated as a primary design consideration. Observability is built in through structured logging, monitoring and alerting to provide clear visibility of service health, performance and security events. Resilience is supported through automated recovery, health checks and scalable architecture aligned to NRW’s sustainability objectives. Operational readiness is established through runbooks, support models and defined service ownership, with ongoing compliance monitoring embedded as part of live service management. Continuous improvement is driven by telemetry and user feedback, enabling iterative refinement of reliability, usability and security.
Knowledge transfer and capability uplift are embedded throughout delivery. Blended teams, paired working and joint backlog ownership ensure NRW staff participate directly in design, build and operation. This is reinforced through structured documentation, communities of practice and targeted coaching across engineering, operations and security disciplines. Responsibility is transitioned progressively, enabling NRW to take full ownership with confidence. The outcome is a secure, resilient service supported by an in-house team capable of sustaining and evolving the platform independently.

Section 4 – Scenario 2 – NRW Website Innovation Prototype
Proposed approach and sequencing for a 12-week innovation window
The types of outcomes and deliverables that could be reasonably achieved within 12 weeks
Key risks, dependencies and assumptions
High-level team structure and capability mix appropriate to this innovation window
This 12-week website innovation prototype should be understood as a structured innovation window designed to reduce uncertainty, not as a miniature delivery project using IBM Garage (IBM Garage). The purpose is to test whether targeted changes to NRW’s website can materially improve usability, accessibility, content effectiveness and service outcomes within the constraints of the current estate, including Umbraco CMS, Microsoft 365 authoring workflows, Azure-hosted services and APIs via Azure API Manager, and identity through Entra ID and Azure AD B2C. The website is therefore the validation environment rather than the final product.
The approach is hypothesis-led. At the outset, NRW and IBM agree a small number of testable hypotheses linked to identifiable user or organisational problems. These typically relate to navigation friction, search relevance, accessibility barriers, bilingual content usability, or the presentation of live and location-based information. Each hypothesis is defined so it can be tested through evidence rather than opinion. The intent is not to generate ideas, but to determine which changes create measurable value and are feasible within NRW’s constraints. For example, a hypothesis may focus on improving alignment to the Digital Service Standards for Wales and WCAG 2.2 AA through bilingual, user-centred prototypes and proportionate measurement of usability and accessibility outcomes.
This aligns with the Digital Service Standard for Wales and embeds user-centred design, accessibility and bilingual considerations throughout. It supports NRW’s objective of exploring improvement within a defined period without committing to a full rebuild.
A critical aspect is that NRW remains actively involved throughout. Stakeholders participate in defining problems, agreeing success criteria, reviewing evidence and making decisions on whether to continue, adapt or stop. The value of the 12-week window lies in enabling better decisions, not simply producing artefacts.
The work is structured around three innovation modes depending on the nature of the challenge.
Discovery-led innovation is applied where the problem is not fully understood and the initial task is identifying viable opportunities.
Proof-led innovation is used where a defined idea requires validation within the existing architectural, data and security context.
Optimisation-led innovation focuses on known pain points where the objective is to improve a measurable baseline.
Across all modes, the operating pattern remains consistent. A hypothesis is defined, a lightweight experiment is designed, the experiment is run in a controlled way, evidence is gathered, and a decision is made on whether to continue, adapt or stop. The emphasis is on rapid learning and disciplined disposal of weak ideas rather than progressing a fixed backlog.
The team combines IBM delivery capability, Stable’s Welsh-based expertise and NRW participation. The core team includes product, delivery and technical leadership, supported by UX and content design, engineering, and data or AI capability where required. This is complemented by embedded NRW roles, including sponsor, subject matter experts, content owners and access to representative users. These roles ensure decisions are timely and grounded in operational reality.
Rather than structuring the 12 weeks as fixed sprints, the work progresses through four phases of evidence generation:
Explore & Discover establishes the problem and defines hypotheses through analysis of user journeys, analytics, search behaviour, accessibility issues and bilingual content considerations. The output is a small number of explicit hypotheses with defined success measures and evidence requirements.
Ideate/Co-create focuses on low-cost experimentation, including clickable prototypes, revised content structures and search concepts. The objective is to invalidate weak ideas early and identify those that justify further investment.
Design and Build/Co-execute deepens the strongest candidates through more representative prototypes, controlled integration with live or near-live data, and strengthened accessibility and bilingual testing. Where appropriate, selected changes may be exposed to real users. The aim is to distinguish between ideas that are interesting and those that are workable within NRW’s environment.
Scale/Co-operate consolidates evidence into a decision-ready position. For each hypothesis, NRW receives a clear view of what was tested, what evidence was generated, whether the hypothesis is supported, what constraints exist and what the recommended next step should be. This may lead to scale, refinement or cessation.
Where proportionate, limited live deployment may be used to validate behaviour under real conditions rather than relying solely on workshop feedback.
Outputs at the end of the 12 weeks are practical and decision oriented. These include validated hypotheses, reusable design and content patterns, and MVP components with proven technical feasibility. Supporting artefacts include:
User-centred and bilingual design outputs, including prioritised journeys, content patterns and accessibility considerations aligned to WCAG 2.2 AA
Navigation and information architecture prototypes addressing complexity and inconsistency, aligned to Umbraco and Microsoft 365 content models
Evidence-led search improvements, including metadata and UX changes to improve relevance
End-to-end prototypes for priority user journeys, demonstrating measurable improvements in usability and task completion
A prioritised accessibility and content improvement backlog, including bilingual guidance and design system adjustments
A lightweight proof of feasibility for live data integration using Azure APIs and identity patterns aligned to governance constraints
A structured content model and authoring approach to improve consistency and findability
A measurement framework defining success metrics such as search effectiveness, task completion and bilingual parity
High-level technical considerations and a sequenced backlog for scaling beyond the innovation window
Key risks are managed through the design of the innovation window. Poorly defined objectives are addressed through early hypothesis definition. Limited user access is mitigated by securing stakeholder and user engagement upfront. Technical and content constraints are addressed through early feasibility testing. The discipline to stop weak ideas protects time and investment for higher-value opportunities.
This approach assumes access to stakeholders, representative users, analytics and relevant technical and content constraints. It also assumes that the purpose of the 12-week window is to generate evidence for future action rather than deliver a production-ready service.
Framed in this way, the innovation prototype becomes a controlled mechanism for learning. It enables NRW to test ideas credibly, fail safely where necessary, and move forward only where there is sufficient evidence that the outcome justifies scaling.

Section 6 – Commercial and Pricing Approach (Framework)
Suppliers must submit a competed DDaT ISIP Pricing.xls and provide a written commercial summary explaining:
The proposed Pricing structure under the framework (e.g. time and materials, fixed price where applicable)
Key assumptions underpinning the submitted rate card
How any proposed volume discounts would operate in practice
IBM supports flexible commercial models that allow public sector organisations to select the most appropriate pricing approach depending on the nature, maturity and certainty of each requirement, indeed different delivery stages potentially benefit from different commercial models.
Time and Materials arrangements are often appropriate during discovery or early exploration where requirements are still evolving. This model provides flexibility to scale effort as insight emerges while ensuring transparency around effort and cost.
Fixed Price delivery can provide greater financial predictability where scope, outcomes and acceptance criteria are sufficiently clear. This is useful where requirements are stable and the supplier is best placed to manage the associated delivery risk.
Fixed Capacity delivery, such as a defined number of delivery sprints or a stable team for a fixed period, provides a balance between agility and cost certainty. This model works well where priorities may shift but the budget envelope needs to remain controlled.
Supplier-led delivery models can be appropriate for ongoing operational services such as managed platforms, DevOps, support or infrastructure services, where predictable service charges and agreed service levels are more suitable than project-based charging.
Blended delivery models combining buyer and supplier teams are often particularly effective. These allow specialist expertise to be introduced where required while maintaining internal ownership, building capability and encouraging shared delivery accountability.
The rate card submitted for the framework reflects the typical market roles required for digital, data and technology delivery, including architecture, engineering, delivery management, design, data and security capabilities. At ISIP stage, pricing is structured to expose the main cost drivers and assumptions so that commercial realism can be discussed during dialogue.
Key assumptions underpinning the rate card include standard role definitions, innovation fund contribution, inclusive of all delivery costs, an 8-hour working day, normal delivery environments and framework-level delivery assumptions. Where call-off requirements differ significantly, for example due to specialist assurance needs, unusual mobilisation requirements or sustained volume demand, rates and delivery models may be refined through the appropriate call-off process.
Volume discounts can operate where sustained delivery volumes allow efficiencies to be achieved through team stability, reduced onboarding friction and longer-term planning. These efficiencies can be passed through via reduced blended rates or agreed discount structures.
Overall, the commercial approach is intended to provide NRW with flexibility, transparency and value for money across the DDaT lifecycle, while supporting the range of delivery models envisaged in the framework documentation. It also aligns to the commercial positions already described in our earlier market engagement response, particularly the use of phased models that evolve as requirement certainty increases.

The intent is to establish an equitable model where contributors see value either directly through funded initiatives or indirectly through improvements delivered across the wider service ecosystem. This can be achieved through a structured approach incorporating the following principles.
Clear and transparent eligibility. All contributing suppliers are able to submit proposals, with eligibility criteria and submission processes documented, published and subject to periodic review. This provides a consistent and accessible route for participation.
Balanced access to funds. Guardrails such as caps on annual funding per supplier, alongside prioritisation for those who have not received recent awards, help prevent disproportionate allocation while still enabling high-impact proposals to progress.
Impact-based funding decisions. Proposals are assessed on their ability to deliver value across the framework rather than benefiting a single supplier’s position. Priority is given to initiatives that improve customer experience, quality, efficiency, sustainability or shared processes, ensuring broader benefit.
Support for collaborative innovation. Joint proposals involving multiple suppliers are encouraged. This supports more even distribution of benefit while enabling shared learning, knowledge transfer and co-development of solutions.
Transparent governance and decision-making. A joint governance group, including customer representatives and independent oversight, is responsible for funding decisions. Criteria, scoring, outcomes and rationale are visible to all contributors, ensuring decisions are accountable and aligned to framework objectives.
Annual equity and value review. A periodic review compares contributions with realised benefits. Where imbalances emerge, adjustments or additional opportunities are introduced, with outcomes shared openly to maintain trust.
Through this approach, NRW can ensure the innovation fund delivers value across all contributors while maximising customer benefit and overall framework performance.
